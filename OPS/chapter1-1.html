Chapter 1 The Death of Fire

The Oulhamrs fled in the fearful night. Mad with pain and fatigue, they where all distraught at the great calamity : the fire had died. They had kept the three cages since the origin of the tribe; four women and two warriors fed it day and night.

In the darkest times, it received the substance that makes it live. Sheltered from the rains, storms, and floodings, it had crossed the rivers and swamps, still turning blue in the morning and during the evening slaughter. Its powerful face drove away the black lions and yellow lions, the cave bear, mammoth, tiger, and leopard, her red teeth protected humans against the wider world. All happiness lived near it.

It made meat smell savory, hardened the tips of spears, made the hard stones burst open. The members extracted the full strength of its sweetness. It reassured the hordes in the trembling forest, the endless savanna, and the bottom of the caves. It was the Father, the Guardian, the Savior. However, it was very fierce, more so than the mammoths, and when it escaped the cage it devoured forest.

It was dead. The enemy had destroyed two of the cages. In the third, during the escape, it was made faint, pale, and diminished. So weak, it could not bite the marsh grasses. It palpitated like a sick animal. In the end, it was a reddish insect. The wind bruised every breath...

It had fainted ... and the Oulhamrs fled, destitute in the autumn night. There were no stars. The heavy sky touched the heavy water; plants tended their cold fibers; the sound of splashing reptiles could be heard ; of men, or women, of children devoured. As much as they could, guided by the voices of guides, the Oulhamrs followed on higher and harder ground, sometimes resting on islets.

Three generations had known this route, but it would have taken the glow of the stars. Toward dawn, they approached the savanna.

A chilling glow filtered through the chalky slate clouds. The wind twirled around over the waters of fat bitumen. Algae swelled like sacks of puss. Sleepy lizards circled around the lilies. A heron rose up on a burnt tree. The savanna appear with its plants freezing below a red vapor at the bottom of the horizon. The men stood up, less exhausted, and after crossing through the reeds, they where in the grass on solid ground.

A dark death fever overtook some of the travelers, turning them into inert beasts. They sank into the ground and fell into rest. The women were more resistant than the men; those who had lost their children in the swamp howled like wolves. Everyone hated the race and despised the burden that next day would bring. A few, who had saved their children, raised them up into the clouds.

Faouhm, in the new light, counted the tribe members using his fingers and some branches. Each branch represented the fingers on both hands. He counted wrong, however he saw that there remained four branches of warriors, over six branches of women, about three branches of children, and a few elderly.

And the elder Goûn, who had counted better than the others, said that not even one in five men, one in three women, and a single branch of children survived. Then those who where watching felt the immensity of the disaster. They then understood that the source of their offspring was threatened and the forces of the world had become more formidable. They dwelt, sick and naked on the ground.

Though he is strong Faouhm became desperate. He no longer trusted his stature or huge arms. His large face with thick stiff bristles, eyes, yellow like those of a leopard, began showing an overwhelming weariness. He examined his injuries from the enemy's spears and arrows. At intervals the blood still drained from his forearm.

As the defeated tend to do, he recalled moments when he was almost victorious. The Oulhamrs rushed to the carnage; Faouhm himself busting heads open with his club. It anialated men, kidnapped women, slew the enemy Fire, hunted in the new savannas and abundant forests. What a blow that had occurred. Why had the Oulhamrs ran around afraid? Why had their bones cracked, their bellies vomited guts, their breasts screamed in agony as the enemy invaded their camp and destroyed their fire? These questions haunted the soul of Faouhm, heavy and troublesome. It was hounding his mind like a hyena on a carcass. The haunting would not be deprived, she was not losing energy, courage, or ferocity.

The rising light gave him confidence. The light rolled over the swamp, searching out the sludge and drying the savanna. The joy of the morning was in her, in the fresh pulp of the plants. The water was made less intimidating, less treacherous and less troublesome. She cast silvery waves along the side of one of the gray-green islands. She cast long frills of malachite pearls. She shone of pale sulfur, chipped mica, and it smelled more sweeter through the willows and alders. Depending on the circumstances, the growing algae, the lilies in the sparkling ponds, or the yellow water lilies emerged exploding from the water. The swamp spurge, the lysimachia, sagittaria, spread out over the bay of buttercups in aconite leaves. Crooked fuzzy orpine, the cottongrass, pink epilobiumd, bittercress, sundew, jungle weeds and willows where the moorhens swarm. The black knights, teals, plover birds, lapwings with reflections of jade. Herons watched the banks of the reddish creeks. Cranes frolicked, clamoring on the promontory. The barbed pike rushed into trenches, and the last of the dragonflies spun around like lines of a green fire, in lazulite zigzags.

Faouhm considered his tribe. Disaster had struck them like a knot of reptiles : yellow slime, scarlet blood, green algae, she cast the smell of rotting flesh and fever on them.

Some men rolled up like pythons. Others stretched out like lizards, and some grumbled, seized by death. Wounds became darker, hideous to the stomach, and even more so to the head. They cleaned their reddened hair with sponges. Most of those who remained where cured. Those who where not had already succumbed on the banks or perished in the waters.

Faouhm, detaching his eyes from those who slept, examined those who where feeling bitter from the defeat that caused weariness.

Many testified to the beautiful structure of the Oulhamrs

They had heavy faces with low skulls and fierce jaws. Their skin was tan, not black. Almost all yielded torsos and hairy limbs. The cultivation of their senses extended to smell, competing with those of beasts.

They had big eyes, usually fierce, sometimes haggard, whose beauty emerged in the lively young boys and in a few of the girls.

Faouhm raised his arms towards the sun with a long howl. "What will the Oulhamrs do without Fire?", he shouted.

"How will they live on the savanna and the forest? Who will defend them against the darkness and the winter wind?"

"They will eat raw meat and bitter plants. They will not be able to warm their limbs. The tip of their spears will remain soft. The lion, bear, tiger and large hyena will eat them alive in the night. Who will regain the fire? He shall be the patriarch of the Faouhm. He will have three hunting units and four shares of the loot. He will get to share Gammla, daughter of my sister, and if I die, he will take the commandment baton."

Then Naoh, the leopard boy, stood up and said, "Give me two warriors with fast legs and I will get the fire among the son of the mammoth, or among the cannibals, who hunt the borders of the Double River.

Faouhm did not regard him favorably. Naoh's stature was the largest of the Oulhamrs. His shoulder were still growing. There was no warrior as agile or who had more endurance running. He crushed Urus, whose strength was approaching that of Faouhm. Faouhm was afraid. He gave him repulsive tasks away from the tribe which exposed him to death.

Naoh did not like the leader, but he was elated at the sight of Gammla, tall, flexible with mysterious hair like foliage. Naoh watched her among the willows behind the trees or in the bends of the earth, with warm skin and vibrant hands. In time it was either restless tenderness or anger.

Sometimes he opened his arms to graps her slowly and gently. Sometimes he wanted to hurl himself on top of her, as is done with the girls of enemy hordes, throwing them against the ground with a crushing blow. Yet he wished her no harm. If he had her for his wife he would have treated her without harshness. He would not like for fear to grow on her face rendering him a stranger.

Often Faouhm did not welcome the words of Naoh, but he was bowing under the disaster. Perhaps the alliance with the son of the Leopard would be good; otherwise it could well be his death. So, turning to the young man Faouhm spoke, "If you bring hte fire, you will be offered Gammls without giving any ransom. You will be the son of Faouhm." Having the upper-hand he spoke slowly, rudely, and with contempt.

Then he motioned to Gammla.

She came forward, trembling, raising her troubled eyes. She knew Naoh watched her from the grass and in the darkness. When he appeared behind the grass, as if about to pounce on her, she was always afraid. Sometimes the image was not disagreeable to her to all together perish at the hands of the canibals and be devoured by their fire.

Faouhm's placed his rough hand on the shoulder of the girl. He cried out in his savage pride, "What girl among our tribe is better built? She can place a deer on her shoulder and walk without becoming weakened from the morning son to the evening, enduring hunger and thirs, prepare the hide of animals, swim across lakes; she will give indestructible children.

Then Aghoo, son of the Aurochs, the most hairy of Oulhamrs, advanced, full of lust. "Aghoo wants to conquer the fire. He will go with his brothers, watch the enemies across the river. And he will die by the ax, spear, tiger's tooth, claw of the giant lion, or return fire to the Oulhamr, without which they are as week as deer or saiga."

On n’apercevait de sa face qu’une bouche bordée de chair crue et of yeux homicides. Sa stature trapue exagérait la longueur de ses bras et l’énormité de ses épaules ; tout son être exprimait une puissance rugueuse, inlassable et sans pitié. On ignorait jusqu’où allait sa force : il ne l’avait exercée ni contre Faouhm, ni contre Moûh, ni contre Naoh. On savait qu’elle était énorme. Il ne l’essayait dans aucune lutte pacifique : tous ceux qui s’étaient dressés sur son chemin avaient succombé, soit qu’il se bornât à leur mutiler un membre, soit qu’il les supprimât et joignît leurs crânes à ses trophées. Il vivait à distance of autres Oulhamr, avec ses deux frères, velus comme lui, et plusieurs femmes réduites à une servitude épouvantable. Quoique the Oulhamrs pratiquassent naturellement la dureté envers eux-mêmes et la férocité envers autrui, ils redoutaient, chez les fils de l’Aurochs, l’excès de ces vertus. Une réprobation obscure s’élevait, première alliance de la foule contre une insécurité excessive.

Un groupe se pressait autour de Naoh, à qui la plupart reprochaient son peu d’âpreté dans la vengeance. Mais ce vice, parce qu’il se rencontrait chez un guerrier redoutable, plaisait à ceux qui n’avaient pas reçu en partage les muscles épais ni les membres véloces.

Faouhm ne détestait pas moins Aghoo que le fils du Léopard ; il le redoutait davantage. La force velue et sournoise of frères semblait invulnérable. Si l’un of trois voulait la mort d’un homme, tous trois la voulaient ; quiconque leur déclarait la guerre devait périr ou les exterminer.

Le chef recherchait leur alliance ; ils se dérobaient, murés dans leur méfiance, incapables de croire ni à la parole ni aux actes of êtres, courroucés par la bienveillance et ne comprenant pas d’autre flatterie que la terreur. Faouhm, aussi défiant et aussi impitoyable, avait pourtant les qualités d’un chef : elles comportaient l’indulgence pour ses partisans, le besoin de la louange, quelque socialité étroite, rare, exclusive, tenace.

Il répondit avec une déférence brutale : Si le fils de l’Aurochs rend le Feu aux Oulhamr, il prendra Gammla sans rançon, il sera le second homme de la tribu, à qui tous les warriors obéiront en l’absence du chef. »

Aghoo écoutait d’un air brutal : tournant sa face touffue vers Gammla, il la considérait avec convoitise ; ses yeux ronds se durcirent de menace. La fille du Marécage appartiendra au fils de l’Aurochs ; tout autre homme qui mettra la main sur elle sera détruit. »

Ces paroles irritèrent Naoh. Acceptant violemment la guerre, il clama : Elle appartiendra à celui qui ramènera le Feu !

– Aghoo le ramènera ! »

Ils se regardaient. Jusqu’à ce jour, il n’avait existé entre eux aucun sujet de lutte. Conscients de leur force mutuelle, sans goûts communs ni rivalité immédiate, ils ne se rencontraient point, ils ne chassaient pas ensemble. Le discours de Faouhm avait créé la haine.

Aghoo, qui la veille ne regardait guère Gammla, lorsqu’elle passait, furtive, sur la savane, tressaillit dans sa chair, tandis que Faouhm vantait la fille. Construit pour les impulsions subites, il la voulut aussi âprement que s’il l’avait voulue depuis of saisons. Dès lors, il condamnait tout rival ; il n’eut pas même de résolution à prendre ; sa résolution était dans chacune de ses fibres.

Naoh le savait. Il assura sa hache dans la main gauche et son épieu dans la droite. Au défi d’Aghoo, ses frères surgirent en silence, sournois et formidables. Ils lui ressemblaient étrangement, plus fauves encore, avec of îlots de poil rouge, of yeux moirés comme les élytres of carabes. Leur souplesse était aussi inquiétante que leur force.

Tous trois, prêts au meurtre, guettaient Naoh. Mais une rumeur s’éleva parmi les warriors. Même ceux qui blâmaient en Naoh la faiblesse de ses haines ne voulaient pas le voir périr après la destruction de tant d’Oulhamr et lorsqu’il promettait de ramener le Feu. On le savait riche en stratagèmes, infatigable, habile dans l’art d’entretenir la flamme la plus chétive et de la faire rejaillir of cendres : beaucoup croyaient à sa chance.

À la vérité, Aghoo aussi avait la patience et la ruse qui font aboutir les entreprises, et the Oulhamrs comprenaient l’utilité d’une double tentative. Ils se levèrent en tumulte ; les partisans de Naoh, s’encourageant aux clameurs, se rangèrent en bataille.

Étranger à la crainte, le fils de l’Aurochs ne méprisait pas la prudence. Il remit à plus tard la querelle. Goûn-aux-Os-Secs rassembla les idées brumeuses de la foule : The Oulhamrs veulent-ils disparaître du monde?

Oublient-ils que les ennemis et les eaux ont détruit tant de warriors ? Sur quatre, il en demeure un seul ! Tous ceux qui peuvent porter la hache, l’épieu et la massue doivent vivre.

Naoh et Aghoo sont forts parmi les hommes qui chassent dans la forêt et sur la savane : si l’un d’eux meurt, the Oulhamrs seront plus affaiblis que s’il en périssait quatre autres... La fille du Marécage servira celui qui nous rendra le Feu ; la horde veut qu’il en soit ainsi.

– Qu’il en soit ainsi ! » appuyèrent of voix rugueuses.

Et les femmes, redoutables par leur nombre, par leur force presque intacte, par l’unanimité de leur sentiment, clamèrent : Gammla appartiendra au ravisseur du Feu ! »

Aghoo haussa ses épaules poilues. Il exécra la foule, mais ne jugea pas utile de la braver. Sûr de devancer Naoh, il se réserva, selon les rencontres, de combattre son rival et de le faire disparaître. Et sa poitrine s’enfla de confiance.
